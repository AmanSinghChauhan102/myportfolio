Introduction to Matter

Definition: "Anything that has mass and occupies space is called matter".

MASS + SPACE = "MATTER"

Examples: Table, chair, air, water, honey, etc.

Classification: Matter is classified based on its physical and chemical properties.

Physical Nature of Matter

Particulate Nature: Matter is composed of tiny particles, which are called atoms.

Size of Particles:

  • The particles are incredibly small, beyond our imagination.
  • They are so small they cannot be seen with the naked eye or a regular microscope.
  • Powerful microscopes can be used to observe them.

Activity (Demonstrating Particle Size):

Experiment: Dissolve 2-3 potassium permanganate crystals in 100 mL of water. Then, take 10 mL of this solution and add it to 90 mL of clear water. This process is repeated 5 to 8 times.

Observation: The water remains colored even after many dilutions.

Conclusion: This shows that just a few crystals contain millions of tiny particles, which are able to color a large volume of water.

Characteristics of Particles of Matter

Summary of Characteristics:

  • The particles of matter are very, very small.
  • The particles of matter have space between them.
  • The particles of matter are constantly moving.
  • The particles of matter attract each other.

A. Particles of Matter Have Spaces Between Them

The space between particles varies in different states of matter; it is least in solids and most in gases.

Example: When sugar or salt is dissolved in water, the particles occupy the interspaces between the water particles, and as a result, the water level does not change.

B. Particles of Matter are in Constant Motion

Particles of matter are always moving. The extent of movement varies by state:

  • Solids: Particles vibrate in fixed positions (least movement).
  • Liquids: Particles can slide past one another (more movement).
  • Gases: Particles move freely at high speeds (most movement).

Kinetic Energy: The energy that particles possess due to their motion is called Kinetic Energy.

Diffusion: It is the process of a substance mixing with another due to the movement of its particles. Diffusion is fastest in gases and slowest in solids. Increasing the temperature raises the kinetic energy of particles, making them move faster and thus increasing the rate of diffusion.

Examples: The smell of perfume or hot food spreads because their particles diffuse through the air. The particles from a burning incense stick move rapidly and mix with air to reach every part of a room.

C. Particles of Matter Attract Each Other

An inter-particle force of attraction exists between the particles of every substance. To break a substance, this force must be overcome. The strength of this force varies between different substances.

The physical state of matter is determined by the balance between the kinetic energy of particles and the inter-particle force of attraction.

Example: It is easy to move your hand through air (negligible force) and water (weak force), but it is difficult to move it through a solid plank of wood (very strong force).

States of Matter

Matter exists in three primary states: Solids, Liquids, and Gases.

Solids

Fixed shape and volume

Strong intermolecular forces

Examples: Ice, iron

Liquids

Fixed volume, takes container shape

Moderate intermolecular forces

Examples: Water, oil

Gases

No fixed shape or volume

Weak intermolecular forces

Examples: Air, helium

Other States of Matter

Plasma: Considered the fourth state of matter. It is a high-energy, ionized state where atoms are stripped of their electrons, creating a mix of free electrons and ions.

  • Natural Plasma: The sun and stars are made of plasma created by high temperatures.
  • Man-Made Plasma: Used in neon signs, where an electric current ionizes gas to create plasma that emits light.

Bose-Einstein Condensate (BEC): The fifth state of matter, based on a study by Indian physicist Satyendra Nath Bose and predicted by Albert Einstein. It is formed by cooling a gas of extremely low density to super-low temperatures.

Can Matter Change Its State?

Yes, matter can change its state by altering conditions of temperature and pressure.

A. Effect of Temperature

Melting (Solid to Liquid): Increasing the temperature of a solid increases the kinetic energy of its particles, causing them to vibrate faster. Eventually, the heat energy overcomes the forces of attraction, and the solid converts to a liquid. This process is also known as fusion.

Melting Point: The minimum temperature at which a solid melts into a liquid. The melting point of ice is 273.15 K (0°C).

Boiling (Liquid to Gas): Heating a liquid increases particle movement.

Boiling Point: The temperature at which a liquid starts boiling to become a gas at atmospheric pressure. The boiling point of water is 373 K (100°C).

Boiling is a bulk phenomenon, meaning particles from the entire bulk of the liquid gain enough energy to change state.

Latent Heat: During a change of state (like melting or boiling), the temperature remains constant. The heat energy supplied is used to overcome the forces of attraction between particles. This hidden heat is called latent heat.

  • Latent Heat of Fusion: The amount of heat needed to convert 1 kg of a solid into a liquid at its melting point.
  • Latent Heat of Vaporization: The amount of heat needed to convert 1 kg of a liquid into a gas at its boiling point.

Sublimation: The change of state directly from solid to gas without passing through the liquid state. Examples include ammonium chloride, camphor, naphthalene, and dry ice (solid CO₂).

Deposition: The change of state directly from gas to solid.

B. Effect of Pressure

The physical state of matter can be changed by increasing or decreasing pressure.

Gases can be converted into liquids by increasing pressure and lowering temperature.

Solids can be changed into gases by decreasing the pressure.

Dry Ice: Solid carbon dioxide (CO₂) is stored under high pressure. When the pressure is decreased, it directly converts into carbon dioxide gas.

Evaporation

Definition: The phenomenon of a liquid changing into vapors at any temperature below its boiling point.

How Evaporation Causes Cooling:

During evaporation, particles of a liquid absorb energy from their surroundings to overcome the forces of attraction and change into vapor. This absorption of heat makes the surroundings cool.

Examples: We sweat in summer, and the evaporation of this sweat absorbs heat from our body, making us feel cool. Cotton clothes are good absorbers of sweat and expose it to the air, which increases the rate of evaporation. Water kept in an earthen pot stays cool because water evaporates from the pores on its surface.

Factors Affecting Evaporation

  • Temperature: Rate of evaporation increases with an increase in temperature.
  • Surface Area: Rate of evaporation increases with an increase in surface area.
  • Humidity: Rate of evaporation decreases as humidity increases.
  • Wind Speed: Rate of evaporation increases with an increase in wind speed.

Comparison: Evaporation vs. Boiling

Evaporation Boiling
Can take place at all temperatures Takes place only at a fixed temperature (the boiling point)
A slow and gradual process A rapid and violent process
Takes place only at the surface of the liquid Takes place over the entire liquid
Causes cooling Does not produce cooling

Important SI Units & Conversions

  • Mass: The SI unit is the kilogram (kg).
  • Volume: The SI unit is the cubic meter (m³). A common unit is the liter (L). 1L = 1dm³; 1L = 1000mL; 1mL = 1cm³.
  • Temperature: The SI unit is Kelvin (K). Other units are degree Celsius (°C) and Fahrenheit.

Celsius to Kelvin: K = °C + 273.15 (often rounded to 273)

Kelvin to Celsius: °C = K - 273.15 (often rounded to 273)

Examples: 0°C = 273K, 25°C = 298K, 100°C = 373K.

Questions from the Document

What about a rubber band, can it change its shape on stretching? Is it a solid?

A rubber band is considered a solid because it has a definite shape and volume. When stretched, it changes shape temporarily due to its elastic properties, but it returns to its original shape when the force is removed.

What about sugar and salt? When kept in different jars these take the shape of the jar. Are they solid?

Yes, sugar and salt are solids. They are composed of small crystals that can flow and take the shape of their container like liquids, but each individual crystal maintains its solid state with a definite shape and volume.

What about a sponge? It is a solid yet we are able to compress it. Why?

A sponge is a solid with a porous structure containing many air pockets. When compressed, the air is squeezed out, allowing the sponge to reduce in volume. However, the solid material itself is not compressed significantly.

When a Solid melts its temperature remains the same, so where does the heat energy go?

This heat gets used up in changing the state by overcoming the forces of attraction between the particles.

Why should we wear cotton clothes in summer?

In summer, we sweat more. Cotton clothes absorb this sweat and expose it to the air, which helps in its evaporation and cools our body.

Why do we see water droplets on the outer surface of a glass containing ice-cold water?

Water vapor present in the air comes in contact with the cold outer surface of the glass and condenses, turning into liquid water droplets.
